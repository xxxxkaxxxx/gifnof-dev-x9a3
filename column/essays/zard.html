ZARD, The Queen of the 1990s

A voice from the 90s that still echoes in my life.

The 1990s saw the rise of a wide variety of artists. Singer-songwriters, bands, idols—there was an incredible diversity in style and form.

But as is true in any era, success is never guaranteed.

Some artists debut, only to fade into obscurity. Some shine briefly with a single hit before disappearing. Others achieve a few successes, yet are eventually swept away by time.

To survive as an artist is, without question, an incredibly difficult challenge.

And yet, in such a competitive world, there are those who not only created numerous hit songs but also left a lasting mark on people’s memories. ZARD is one of them.

Early Days

ZARD originally began as a group name, a band centered around vocalist Izumi Sakai.

However, the structure soon changed. ZARD gradually evolved into a solo project led by Sakai.

This shift proved to be a major turning point, as she quickly rose to the forefront of the era.

Their debut single, “Good-bye My Loneliness”, was tied to a television drama and sold around 200,000 copies.

The song was composed by Tetsuro Oda, who would later play a key role in many of ZARD’s hits. Masahiro Kuribayashi was also involved, and together they formed the core of ZARD’s sound.

In other words, the foundation of ZARD’s musical identity was already established from the very beginning.

Arrangers such as Takeshi Hayama and Masao Akashi also contributed, and their names continued to appear in album credits throughout ZARD’s peak years.

By their second album, songs like “Fushigi ne…” and “Mou Sagasanai” already showcased what would become the signature ZARD sound. The album eventually surpassed 300,000 copies in sales.

Million-Selling Era

Following the success of their previous work, ZARD released their third album, HOLD ME.

Tracks like “Nemurenai Yoru wo Daite” and “Ano Hohoemi wo Wasurenai de” highlight their signature pop style.

Songs such as “Konna ni Aishitemo” echo earlier works, while consistently expressing relatable themes of love.

One standout is “Tooi Hi no Nostalgia”. Its opening piano and melancholic minor IV chord evoke a deep sense of distant memories.

Though not composed by Oda or Kuribayashi, the song still carries the unmistakable ZARD sound, thanks to arranger Masao Akashi.

From this album onward, ZARD achieved six consecutive million-selling records.

Signature Songs

In 1993, the album Yureru Omoi was released, featuring what would become ZARD’s defining songs.

The title track was used in a commercial, and its refreshing sound perfectly matched the product’s image.

Then there is “Makenaide”, widely recognized as one of Japan’s greatest cheer songs.

Personally, I would place it alongside “Nandodemo” by DREAMS COME TRUE and “Tomorrow” by Mayo Okamoto.

Both songs were composed by Tetsuro Oda, further highlighting his central role in ZARD’s success.

Their next album, OH MY LOVE, was created by a powerful team of Oda and Kuribayashi, with all arrangements handled by Akashi. It went on to surpass two million copies in sales.

Continued Momentum

ZARD’s momentum continued in the following years.

On forever you, Kuribayashi delivered notable work, including “High Heel wo Nugisutete”, a beautifully structured heartbreak song.

Albums like TODAY IS ANOTHER DAY and Eien featured numerous anime tie-ins.

“DAN DAN Kokoro Hikareteku”, used in Dragon Ball GT, features a powerful ♭VI–♭VII–I progression.

“Unmei no Roulette Mawashite” was used in Detective Conan, further expanding ZARD’s reach.

ZARD’s music connected with all audiences— through love songs, encouragement, and anime themes alike.

The End

After this period of success, album sales began to decline.

As the 2000s arrived, music trends shifted, and ZARD’s pop-oriented sound gradually fell out of sync with the times.

The rise of digital distribution also weakened the CD market, creating challenges for many artists.

ZARD was no exception.

Eventually, Izumi Sakai passed away during her battle with illness.

A Symbol of an Era

Within just ten years, ZARD became a household name.

Even now, songs like “Yureru Omoi” and “Makenaide” continue to stay with me.

Had she lived, what kind of music would she have created today? We will never know.

But ZARD’s music will always remain a part of my playlist.
