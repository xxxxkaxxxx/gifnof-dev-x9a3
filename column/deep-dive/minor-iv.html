The Power of the Ⅳm

Songs Where Ⅳm Appears

Have you ever listened to a song and felt a sudden sense of melancholy, without fully knowing why?

Is it the lyrics that create that feeling? Or is it the emotion that emerges when we project ourselves into the words? Of course, those elements play a significant role.

Many factors contribute to emotional impact in music. But this time, I would like to focus on one specific harmonic device: the minor iv chord (Ⅳm).

In the J-pop I have listened to over the years, there are countless songs where ivm appears in striking ways. For now, let’s focus on one example.

▶
Miyuki Nakajima – “Jidai” (Time Goes Around)
ivm enters around 1:14

What did you notice? In each of these songs, ivm is placed in a way that subtly alters the atmosphere, often introducing a brief sense of shadow or fragility.

How Ⅳm Appears in Common Progressions

I → IV → Ⅳm → I

The progression moves from tonic to subdominant, then briefly shifts to iv before returning home. It is as if a momentary shadow passes through an otherwise bright landscape.

I → Ⅳm → I → Ⅳm

This progression appears at the beginning of “Owarinaki Tabi” by Mr.Children. By moving back and forth between I and Ⅳm, the harmony creates a subtle tension between stability and emotional unease.

Mr.Children – “Owarinaki Tabi”

I → IVm → I → IVm at the beginning.

Ⅱm → Ⅲm → Ⅳm

The harmony ascends toward what sounds like a dominant destination, yet instead of resolving conventionally, it often returns to I. Even the rhythmic phrasing in such songs tends to resemble one another.

ivm Appearing Alongside the Lyrics

Due to copyright restrictions, I cannot reproduce the actual lyrics here. However, there are moments when the appearance of ivm coincides with a lyrical shift, and the emotional effect becomes noticeably stronger.

Simply assigning “sad lyrics” to minor chords and “happy lyrics” to major chords would weaken the message. The key lies in balance.

For example, in GLAY’s “Pure Soul,” a repeated phrase appears twice. The first time, it is harmonized with IV. The second time, with Ⅳm.

GLAY – “pure soul”

IV → IVm contrast in the repeated phrase.

The first statement feels accepting. The second feels unresolved — as if acceptance has become something that must be endured. (This is, of course, a personal interpretation.)

More recently, Official HIGE DANdism’s “Pretender” also makes effective use of ivm. When paired with the lyrics, its impact becomes unmistakable.

Closing Thoughts

The songs mentioned here are compelling for many reasons beyond ivm alone. Still, its placement may contribute to an intensification of emotion.

Next time you listen to a song, try paying attention to the moment when Ⅳm appears. You may begin to hear it differently.
